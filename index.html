Health Risks of Poor Indoor Air
Lung
Respiratory Issues: High PM2.5 indoors can cause asthma, bronchitis, and reduced lung function over time.
Mind
Cognitive Decline: Studies show poor air quality reduces concentration, memory, and cognitive performance.
Heart
Cardiovascular Stress: Long-term exposure increases risk of heart disease and stroke by 20-30%.
😴
Sleep Disruption: VOCs and CO₂ buildup during sleep affects sleep quality and morning alertness.
🔧 DIY Air Purifier at Home
1
Get a Box Fan (20-inch works best)

A standard household box fan, available for ₹800-1500 at any electronics store.

2
Attach MERV-13 Filter

Secure a 20"x20"x1" MERV-13 furnace filter to the back of the fan with tape. Cost: ₹600-1200.

3
Run Continuously in Occupied Room

Place in the center of room. Can reduce PM2.5 by 70-90%. Replace filter monthly.

CPCB — Central Pollution Control Board Guidelines

Official recommendations from India's apex body for pollution control and environmental standards.

AQI CATEGORY ACTIONS (CPCB)
0–50 GOOD
No health impact. Ideal for outdoor activities for all groups.
51–100 MOD
Minor breathing discomfort to sensitive people. Prolonged outdoor exertion should be limited for sensitive groups.
101–200 POOR
Breathing discomfort to most people on prolonged exposure. People with heart/lung disease, children, elderly should avoid outdoor exertion.
201–300 V.POOR
Respiratory illness on prolonged exposure. Avoid outdoor activities. Wear N95 mask if going out. Keep windows closed.
300+ HAZARD
Health alert — serious respiratory effects on everyone. Stay indoors with all windows closed. Use air purifiers with HEPA filters.
CPCB EMISSION STANDARDS
PM
PM2.5 Annual Standard: 40 μg/m³ (24-hr average: 60 μg/m³). India's NAAQS standard for fine particulate matter.
NO₂
NO₂ Annual Standard: 40 μg/m³. Primarily from vehicle exhaust and industrial combustion.
O₃
Ozone 8-hr Standard: 100 μg/m³. Ground-level ozone worsens during hot, sunny conditions.
CPCB GRADED RESPONSE ACTION PLAN
Stage 1 (AQI 201–300): Mechanical sweeping of roads, strict enforcement of dust norms at construction sites, ban on open waste burning.
Stage 2 (AQI 301–400): Hot-mix plants and stone crushers shut, diesel generators restricted, heightened vehicle pollution checks.
Stage 3 (AQI 401–450): Brick kilns and industries with inadequate controls closed, BS-III/IV diesel vehicles restricted in NCR zones.
Stage 4 (AQI >450): Emergency measures — schools/colleges shift online, construction halted, additional transport restrictions imposed.
WHO — World Health Organization Recommendations

Global air quality guidelines updated in 2021, representing the most health-protective standards.

WHO AIR QUALITY GUIDELINES (2021)
PM2.5 5 μg/m³ annual / 15 μg/m³ 24-hr

Fine particulate matter — most harmful pollutant. These are the strictest globally recommended limits.

PM10 15 μg/m³ annual / 45 μg/m³ 24-hr

Coarse dust particles from roads, construction, and industrial sources.

NO₂ 10 μg/m³ annual / 25 μg/m³ 24-hr

Nitrogen dioxide from traffic and combustion. Linked to respiratory disease.

Ozone (O₃) 60 μg/m³ 8-hr peak season

Ground-level ozone formed from vehicle and industrial emissions in sunlight.

WHO HEALTH PROTECTION ACTIONS
Reduce exposure time: WHO advises limiting time outdoors when AQI exceeds 100. Reschedule vigorous outdoor exercise to low-pollution periods (early morning or after rain).
Indoor air protection: Use HEPA air purifiers, avoid smoking indoors, reduce candle or incense burning, and ensure proper kitchen ventilation to reduce indoor PM2.5.
Respiratory protection: N95/FFP2 respirators filter 95% of airborne particles. Regular surgical masks offer limited protection against PM2.5.
High-risk groups: WHO identifies pregnant women, infants, children under 5, adults over 65, and those with cardiorespiratory disease as requiring extra protection at all AQI levels.
WHO — MUMBAI CONTEXT
PM2.5 gap: Mumbai's average PM2.5 (~60 μg/m³ annually) is 12× the WHO guideline of 5 μg/m³, representing a major public health burden.
Disease burden: WHO estimates 7 million deaths globally per year from air pollution. Urban India is among the highest-risk regions for chronic respiratory and cardiovascular disease from PM exposure.
